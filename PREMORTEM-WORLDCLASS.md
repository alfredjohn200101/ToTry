# To Try — World-Class Premortem (evidence-grounded)
*Judged through the eyes of the people who'd use and assess it. Criteria, not a victory lap.*

## What the evidence says (and how To Try measures up)

### THE THERAPIST / ADDICTION CLINICIAN
What works, per the research:
- **CBT + ACT lead the evidence** — cognitive restructuring (catch the distorted thought:
  "this relieves stress"), urge surfing / mindfulness (watch a craving arrive without acting),
  and relapse-prevention skill-building. → To Try's companion does urge-surfing well. **GAP: it
  doesn't do explicit cognitive restructuring** — naming the distortion, challenging it, replacing
  it. That's the single most evidence-backed technique and it's thin here.
- **"A lapse is feedback, not failure"** is the one reframe that protects recovery most; rigid
  all-or-nothing abstinence itself becomes a source of distress. → To Try's grace-after-relapse
  nails this philosophically. Strong.
- **Reach UNDER the behavior** — the stress, loneliness, anxiety the vice was managing. → The
  companion gestures at this; could go deeper (the "what was this managing?" question).
- **The hard truth clinicians would say:** an app should be a *bridge to a person*, not the plan
  itself. "Don't let an app be the only thing holding the plan up." → To Try must point to real
  humans (confession, a brother, a counsellor), never position itself as sufficient. It mostly does
  (Magnifica Humanitas) — but should be even more explicit about being a companion *to* real help.

### THE DOCTOR / BEHAVIOURAL-MEDICINE VIEW
- **EMI (Ecological Momentary Intervention)** — help delivered *in the moment and situation it's
  needed* — is the modern standard, and it's exactly To Try's threshold-brother. This is genuinely
  evidence-aligned and ahead of most apps.
- **Craving is the #1 relapse predictor** — managing the craving moment is the highest-value target.
  → The companion + SOS is the right instinct. **GAP: no structured craving log that learns the
  pattern** (time, trigger, mood) and feeds it back as insight. The data exists scattered; it isn't
  yet *intelligence the man can see about himself.*

### THE PRODUCT EXPERT / RETENTION REALITY (the brutal part)
The numbers are sobering and must shape expectations:
- **~70% abandon health apps within 100 days; 30-day retention can be as low as 3–4%.** Mass reach
  is HARD and the bar is mostly UX, not features.
- **Retention failure ≈ 40% UX problems, 35% content inadequacy, 25% design.** Not marketing.
  73% of users say ease-of-use is the single most important factor.
- **Overwhelming feature presentation causes immediate abandonment.** → This is To Try's BIGGEST
  RISK. It does a LOT. A new man opening it could drown. The hero + one-next-step fights this, but
  the sheer surface area is the threat. **Refinement: ruthless first-run simplicity — hide depth
  until earned.**
- **The healthy paradox:** the best mental-health tools "help people get better and move on" —
  episodic use, not daily-habit addiction. → This *aligns* with To Try's anti-engagement ethic.
  Don't chase daily-active-user vanity; measure whether a man's life got better. Monetize/measure
  OUTCOMES, not engagement. (This is philosophically a strength — lean into it openly.)
- **Financial incentives don't improve outcomes; notifications/gamification are "surface
  manipulations" that don't forge lasting bonds.** → Validates To Try's restraint. The bond has to
  be genuine (the brother), not gamified.

### THE MAN IN MY SHOES (putting myself there honestly)
If I were struggling the way you are and opened this:
- **First feeling:** "this is beautiful and it gets me" — the soul lands. GOOD.
- **Second feeling, within a minute:** "...there's a lot here. Where do I start?" — RISK. The five
  tabs + sub-tabs + every screen rich with content could tip from "home for everything" into
  "too much." The line between *comprehensive* and *overwhelming* is the whole game.
- **At 11pm in the craving:** can I get help in ONE tap without thinking? → The orb is always there.
  GOOD. But is the path from orb → relief *fast and dead-simple*, or does it ask me to choose between
  options when my willpower is gone? **Refinement: the crisis path must be the lowest-friction thing
  in the entire app. One tap to a breathing, present voice. No menus.**
- **The morning after a slip:** does it shame me or meet me? → Grace-first. GOOD.
- **Would I keep coming back?** Only if it earns it by being *useful in the moment*, not by nagging.
  The brother-at-threshold is the right bet.

## THE REFINEMENTS THAT WOULD MAKE IT WORLD-CLASS (priority order)
1. **First-run simplicity / progressive disclosure.** A new man sees ONE thing to do, not the whole
   cathedral. Depth unlocks as he walks. (Biggest retention lever per evidence.)
2. **The crisis path = zero-friction.** Orb → immediate present voice, no menu to navigate while
   white-knuckling. The most important 3 seconds in the app.
3. **Cognitive restructuring in the companion** — the one evidence-backed technique that's thin:
   catch the thought, challenge it, replace it. Gently, the brother's way.
4. **Craving intelligence** — turn the scattered urge data into a mirror: "your hardest time is
   ~11pm, usually when you're tired and alone." Self-knowledge is the therapy.
5. **Explicitly a bridge to real humans** — confession, a real brother, a counsellor. Never the plan
   itself. Say it plainly somewhere a man will see it.
6. **"Reach under the behavior"** — when met in the moment, gently ask what the urge is *managing*
   (stress? loneliness?) — the clinician's deeper move.

## IS A PWA ENOUGH? (the honest technical answer)
- **For reach:** PWA = no app-store gatekeeping, instant, linkable, shareable — actually IDEAL for an
  influencer-led launch. A man can be in it 5 seconds after tapping your link. Strength.
- **For feel:** with the native motion/elevation/sheets now in place, it can absolutely feel beyond
  "just a website." The gap vs native is now mostly: push notifications (limited on iOS PWA),
  home-screen install friction, and background reliability.
- **The real limitation:** iOS PWA push is constrained, so the "brother reaching out first at the
  right moment" is harder than native. **Mitigation:** make the in-the-moment (when he opens it)
  experience so strong that outreach matters less — EMI on open, not push. And nail the
  "Add to Home Screen" moment so it lives on his phone like an app.
- **Verdict:** PWA is enough to start, prove the soul, and reach people NOW. If it works, native
  wrappers (or Capacitor) later unlock push + store presence. Don't let native be the thing that
  stops the mission starting.

## CAN IT REACH AND CHANGE AT MASS? (honest)
- **The soul + the integration moat are real differentiators** no single-feature app has. That's the
  shot at standing out in a crowded field.
- **The threat is universal:** ~70% leave. To beat that, the first session must be effortless and
  the crisis moment must be flawless. Those two things, more than any feature, decide mass impact.
- **The ethic is the edge:** in a market full of engagement-farming apps that the research itself
  calls out as harmful, a genuinely non-exploitative "brother that gives you back to your life" is
  both rarer and more trustworthy. That's the influencer story AND the clinical high ground.

## THE TENSION TO HOLD
The app's greatest strength (it does everything, the integrated home) is also its greatest risk
(overwhelm). The whole craft from here is **making something comprehensive feel simple.** Not cutting
the depth — *hiding it until the man is ready for it.* That's the world-class line.
